Autism Definition

Autism is a complex neurodevelopmental disorder defined by the presence of social deficits, abnormalities in communication, presence of stereotyped, repetitive behaviours and a characteristic course. The National Trust for the welfare of persons with autism, cerebral palsy, mental retardation and multiple disabilities Act 1999 defined “Autism as a condition of uneven skill development primarily affecting the communication and social abilities of a person marked by repetitive and ritualistic behaviour”. The definition contains terminology like deficits, abnormality and so on. Philosophically if we try to understand about autism it has a deeper insight and that had clearly defined “Normality”.


Autism VS Autism Spectral Disorders (ASD)

Autism is a complex developmental disorder that significantly affects the individual’s verbal and nonverbal communication and social interaction, and is associated with engagement in restricted, repetitive and stereotypic patterns of behaviour, usually evident before age three (American Psychiatric Association (APA), 2000; Autism Society of America (ASA), 2003; World Health Organization (WHO), 1993). Autism is a lifelong developmental disability (The National Autistic Society, www.autism.org.uk/autism). It is part of Autism Spectrum Disorders (ASD) or Pervasive Developmental Disorders (PDD), refers to a spectrum of five disorders characterized by delays in the development of multiple basic functions including socialization and communication. The other disorders in the group are Asperger disorder, Rett syndrome, Childhood disintegrative, Pervasive developmental disorder – not otherwise specified. The word 'spectrum' is used because, while all people with ASD share three main areas of difficulty (socialization, communication and behaviour disorders), their condition will affect them in very different ways. Some are able to live relatively easy everyday lives; others will require a lifetime of specialist support. Among the group that constitutes ASD, the present study mainly focuses on the children with autism in Indian perspectives.


SOCIAL IMPAIRMENT IN AUTISM

  1. Difficulty with social communication
  2. Difficulty with social interaction
  3. Difficulty with social imagination.

The impact of lack of socialization affect in three main areas, which is visible in almost all people with autism. These are known as the 'triad of impairments' (Bogdashina, 2005). They are:

I. Difficulty with Social Communication

People with autism can be verbal or non verbal and have difficulties with both modes of communication. Some people with autism may not speak (non verbal), or have fairly limited speech. Although they usually understand what other people say to them, they prefer to use alternative means of communication, such as sign language or visual symbols. A non-verbal child might use hands to show his/her interests. Those who are verbal with good language skills may have a peculiar speech pattern. They may have extreme language deficits like total lack of functional language, hard to understand give and take nature of conversations. Some of them possess meaningless repetition of words or phrases, immediate and delayed echolalia (meaningless repetition of words), apparent confusion with spoken language, reversal of pronouns (difficulty in discriminating between use of pronouns I and You) and metaphoric language (inability to form a concept of self). They generally take the literal meaning of the language and think people always mean exactly what they say. For example, while teaching toileting skills, if the instructor ask to ‘open the zip’, the child would try to open the instructor's zip. Then the instructor needs to change his phrase saying ‘open your zip’ to facilitate him understanding the correct meaning. They can also find it difficult to use or understand facial expressions or tone of voice, jokes, sarcasm, common phrases and sayings. For example, the phrase 'It's cool', which people often say when they think that something is good, but strictly speaking, it means that it is a bit cold. Hence, to achieve a better communication with people having autism, a person needs to speak in a clear and consistent way and give the time to process what has been said.

II. Difficulty with Social Interaction

People with autism often have difficulty in recognizing or understanding other people's emotions and feelings, and expressing their own, which can make it more difficult for them to fit in socially. They avoid eye contact and seem to live in their own world, resist to be picked up by elders, almost walked through people as if they were not there. They try to be so distant that no one could reach them or display affection. This results in lack of co-operative play with peers and an inability to understand other people's feelings. They do not understand the unwritten social rules which most of us pick up intuitively. They may stand too close to another person or start an inappropriate subject of conversation. They appear to be insensitive because they do not recognize how someone else is feeling and appear to behave strangely or inappropriately, as it is not always easy for them to express feelings, emotions or needs. Sometimes they possess indecent behaviours like licking any object, laughing and giggling without relevance, etc. Difficulties with social interaction can mean that people with autism find it hard to form friendships. Some may want to interact with other people and make friends, but may be unsure how to proceed.

III. Difficulty with Social Imagination

Social imagination allows us to understand and predict other people's behaviour, make sense of abstract ideas, and to imagine situations outside our immediate daily routine. Difficulties with social imagination mean that people with autism find it hard to understand, interpret and predict other people's thoughts, feelings and actions. It leads to lack of understanding in the concept of danger, e.g. that running on to a busy road poses a threat to them. Generally they engage in imaginative play and activities: children with autism may enjoy some imaginative play but prefer to act out the same scenes each time Difficulties with social imagination should not be confused with a lack of imagination. Many people with autism are very creative and may be, for example, accomplished artists, musicians or writers.

Common Characteristics of Autism

It can be hard to create awareness of autism in the society, as people with the condition do not look disabled. Parents of children with autism often say that other people simply think their child as naughty. Different researchers attempted in different ways to categorize the characteristics of children with autism. In defining autism, Ritvo and Freeman made an attempt and have listed a disturbance of developmental rates or sequences as an essential characteristic (Koegel, et al. 1982), which usually includes.

I. Severe delays in language development

If it does develop, it usually includes peculiar speech patterns of the use of words without attachment to their normal meaning. Those who are able to use language effectively may still use unusual metaphors or speak in a formal and monotonous voice.

II. Severe delays in understanding social relationships

The autistic child often avoids eye contact, resists being picked up, and seems to "tune out" the world around it. This results in a lack of co-operative play with peers, an impaired ability to develop friendships and an inability to understand other people's feelings.

III. Inconsistent Patterns of sensory responses

The child who has autism may appear at times to be deaf and fail to respond to words or other sounds. At other times, the same child may be extremely distressed by an everyday noise such as a vacuum cleaner or a dog's barking. Autistic individuals tend to have a high threshold for pain. They lack sensitivity to cold or heat, or may over-react to any of these. There is also a marked restriction of activity and interests.

IV. Insensitivity to pain

Self-injurious behaviour is a commonly reported problem. Many, not all, autistic children lack perception to pain. They may fall and then bruise or cut themselves quite seriously, but they exhibit little or no reaction to the injury that can cause tissue damage, redness, and open wounds. The most common forms of these behaviours include head-banging, hand-biting, and excessive scratching or rubbing. Self-injury acts as a release and thus, lowers arousal. Some forms of self-injury may be a result of over arousal. In some cases, self-injury may be a form of self-stimulatory, stereotypic behaviour. That is, they are repetitive, ritualistic, which provide the individual with some form of sensory stimulation or arousal. It is commonly remarked that autistic individuals tend to have a high threshold for pain.

V. Repetitive body movements

A person who has autism may perform repetitive body movements, such as hand flicking, twisting, spinning or rocking. The individual may also display repetition by following the same route, the same order of dressing, or the same schedule everyday, etc. If changes occur in these routines, the pre-occupied child or adult usually becomes very distressed. Children identified as autistic exhibit many bizarre, repetitive behaviours and were characterized by impaired sensory perception and communication abilities.

VI. Uneven patterns of intellectual functioning

The individual may have peak skills, e.g. scattered things done well in relation to normal functioning - such as drawing, music, computations in math’s, or memorization of facts with no regard to importance or lack of it. On the other hand, the majority of autistic persons have varying degrees of mental retardation, with only 20% having average or above-average intelligence. A person might, for example, be unable to button cloths yet be capable of intricate mathematical manipulation. Similarly, a person might be able to draw elaborate and masterly pictures but not able to master the rudiments of speech (Selfe, 1977). Autistic persons generally have difficulty in self help behaviours such as dressing, washing, using the toilet, and eating with a knife and fork. This combination of intellectual variations makes autism especially perplexing.

VII. Uneven pattern of development

Characteristics of autism include an uneven pattern of development, a fascination with mechanical objects, a ritualistic response to environmental stimuli, and a resistance to any change in the environment. Some autistic children may also have special abilities.
